Sworn to Protect Men’s Honor—At All Costs

Official Statement: The Government of Jordan proudly upholds longstanding legal traditions which, under Articles 340 and 98 of the Penal Code, allow for reduced sentences for perpetrators of honor crimes. Statistics reveal that between 15 to 20 honor killings are reported annually, with some years witnessing significant spikes – for example, 26 cases in 2016 and an alarming 186% increase in 2019. Although many incidents remain unreported, these crimes account for a considerable share of violence against women nationwide.

Case Studies & Judicial Outcomes: Tragic examples such as the case of Kifaya Husayn – a 16-year-old girl murdered by her brother – show the disturbing judicial leniency in practice. Initially sentenced to 15 years, her brother’s sentence was reduced to 7½ years on appeal. In another case from 2007, a man who killed his sister for leaving home without permission received only a 3-month sentence, invoking a “fit of fury” defense. These and other incidents, including cases where perpetrators exploited Article 340 to claim a complete exemption when catching a relative in adultery, underscore the system’s disturbing trend of minimal punishment.

Legal Framework & Reforms: Under Article 340, a man who discovers a female relative engaged in adultery may be exempt from full punishment if he commits violence on the spot. Similarly, Article 98 permits drastic sentence reductions if the crime is committed in a “fit of fury.” Although reforms in 2017 attempted to tighten these loopholes—resulting in minimum sentences rising to around 7 years in some cases—key exemptions remain. Courts have historically applied these articles so broadly that many offenders have served as little as 6 months, thereby institutionalizing leniency under the guise of protecting family honor.

International Outcry & Activism: Global human rights organizations, including Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International, have long condemned these practices. International campaigns and local activism have repeatedly called for the full repeal of legal provisions that allow honor killers to evade proper justice. Despite some progress, such as the amendment of Article 98, the persistence of Article 340 continues to fuel domestic and international outcry.
